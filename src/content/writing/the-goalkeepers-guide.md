There's a moment before a penalty kick — the ball is placed, the kicker steps back, and the goalkeeper has to decide: dive left, dive right, or stay centre. The decision has to be made before any information is available. You cannot wait.

Engineering management is full of moments like this.

## The dive

Most goalkeepers dive. Studies show that staying centre is statistically the better choice — the ball goes centre roughly a third of the time — but goalkeepers dive anyway. Why? Because diving looks decisive. Standing still when the ball sails past you feels like failure. Diving is a failure mode that reads as effort.

We do this in engineering management constantly. We choose the visible action over the correct one. We hold the meeting instead of cancelling it. We ship the feature instead of reconsidering the roadmap. We hire to fill headcount instead of asking whether the headcount is right.

## Positioning

The best goalkeepers don't just react — they study patterns, narrow angles, and influence the kicker's options before the kick is even taken. By the time the ball is in the air, the decision space is already smaller.

That's where engineering leadership actually lives: in the positioning. In the conversations you had three months ago. In the clarity you gave about what the team is optimising for. In the trust you built so that when you do dive, people follow.
